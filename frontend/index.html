Insulin and Diabetes Development

The pancreas creates insulin; insulin monitors how much glucose is within the blood.

  • Cells can absorb glucose and use it to function and work.
  • When the body does not create an appropriate amount of insulin, glucose stays in the blood and is not used.
  • Glucose in the blood could lead to long-term health conditions, such as Diabetes.
  • In Type 2 diabetes, cells within the body begin to withdraw from the use of insulin.
  • The pancreas would try to create more insulin, but the pancreas may not be able to develop enough to keep up with rising glucose levels.

Diabetes in Hispanic and Latino Communities

In Hispanic and Latino communities, Diabetes tends to have more impact in these populations.

  • Hispanic adults, compared to their non-Hispanic white counterparts, have a 70% chance of being diagnosed with Diabetes.
  • Various factors are significant, such as genomic components, socioeconomic class, and inadequate healthcare (CDC, Office of Minority Health).
  • The prevalence of Diabetes in Hispanic communities is higher than in many other demographic groups, with variations across subgroups.
  • For instance, Mexican Americans and Puerto Ricans have some of the highest rates, whereas Cuban Americans show lower prevalence rates.
  • These differences may stem from diverse genetic factors, cultural dietary practices, and levels of healthcare accessibility within subgroups (American Diabetes Association, CDC).
  • Hispanic populations with certain genomic factors may become prone to an ineffective use of insulin.
  • Without a proper regulatory system of insulin, individuals would be more susceptible to developing type 2 diabetes.
  • Traditional diets rich in carbohydrates and fats, combined with lower physical activity, increase risk.
  • Economic limitations can make it difficult to access healthy food options.

Barriers to Diabetes Management in Hispanic Populations

Poverty rates create an obstacle to affording proper healthcare.

  • A lack of awareness and education regarding Diabetes may lead to significant complications (Office of Minority Health, CDC).
  • Limited health literacy in these communities can impede self-management, as Diabetes requires ongoing lifestyle changes and regular monitoring (American Diabetes Association, Office of Minority Health).
  • Diabetes significantly reduces life expectancy and quality of life due to complications such as neuropathy, cardiovascular disease, and kidney failure.
  • The economic burden of Diabetes is also substantial for Hispanic families, who face high out-of-pocket costs for medications, treatments, and hospitalizations (CDC, Office of Minority Health).
  • Without proper management, high blood sugar over time can cause complications like heart disease, kidney damage, and nerve damage.
